## Running an analysis

You can run analytics from the dashboard or through your AI agents.

### From the dashboard

1. Go to **Data** > **Analytics**
2. Select the table you want to analyze
3. Choose the analysis type (trends, forecasting, anomalies, etc.)
4. Select the columns to include
5. Click **Run Analysis**

### Through agents

Your agents can run analytics as part of their workflow. For example, an agent could automatically run anomaly detection on new transaction data and flag suspicious records for review.

### In automated pipelines

Set up scheduled pipelines that run analytics on a regular basis. For example, a daily pipeline could forecast next week's demand and send the results to your team.

## Understanding your results

Every analysis returns:

- **Clear results** -- The actual findings: predictions, clusters, anomalies, trends, etc.
- **Visualizations** -- Charts and graphs ready to display on your dashboard
- **Confidence scores** -- How confident the model is in its predictions
- **Plain-language explanation** -- An AI-generated summary that explains what the results mean in business terms

<Tip>
  Enable the "Include explanation" option to get an AI-generated narrative that explains the results in plain language. This is especially helpful for sharing insights with non-technical team members.
</Tip>

## Analysis types in detail

### Trend analysis

Determine whether a metric is increasing, decreasing, or stable over time. Get the trend direction, rate of change, and statistical confidence.

**Example:** "Is our average order value trending up or down over the last 6 months?"

### Forecasting

Predict future values based on historical patterns. Specify how far ahead you want to forecast, and the platform generates predictions with confidence intervals.

**Example:** "Forecast daily revenue for the next 14 days."

### Anomaly detection

Scan your data for records that are significantly different from the norm. The platform flags anomalies and tells you what makes them unusual.

**Example:** "Find unusual transactions in the last 30 days."

### Clustering

Group your records into natural segments without predefined categories. The platform automatically determines the optimal number of groups and describes what makes each group distinct.

**Example:** "Segment our customers based on purchase behavior."

### Classification

Predict which category a new record belongs to, based on patterns in your historical data. See prediction confidence and which factors matter most.

**Example:** "Predict churn risk for each customer based on their activity."

### Correlation

Find which metrics are related to each other and how strongly. Identify the top factors driving a particular outcome.

**Example:** "What factors correlate most with customer satisfaction?"

## Automatic model selection

You don't need to choose a specific algorithm. DEHA ONE automatically selects the best approach based on:

- Your data size (TabPFN for small, LightGBM for large)
- The type of analysis you requested
- The nature of your columns (numeric, categorical, time-series)
- For forecasting: seasonality, sample count, and trend signal (StatsForecast vs Prophet vs NeuralForecast)

The platform chooses from multiple algorithms and falls back to alternatives if the first choice doesn't fit your data.

## MLflow model registry

Trained models are registered, versioned, and promoted through a built-in **MLflow** registry:

- **Train** a model from any dataset
- **Register** it with a name and a description
- **Promote** between stages: `Staging`, `Production`, or any custom alias
- **Predict** against any registered version at any stage
- **SHAP explanations** for every prediction (Tree explainer for tree models, Kernel explainer otherwise)
- **A/B test** two model versions in production with weighted traffic

You can have multiple versions of the same model coexisting -- one in production, one in staging being evaluated, and one in development being trained.

## Drift detection

Once a model is in production, monitor it for drift:

- **Input drift** — has the distribution of features shifted vs. the training set?
- **Output drift** — have the predictions shifted vs. the validation set?
- **Concept drift** — has the relationship between inputs and outputs changed?

Statistical tests used:

- **PSI** (Population Stability Index) — production-standard for monitoring
- **Kolmogorov-Smirnov** — non-parametric distribution comparison
- **Jensen-Shannon** divergence — symmetric distribution distance
- **Wasserstein** distance — geometric distance between distributions

Reports come with **severity grading** (green / yellow / red) and an Evidently HTML report you can open in the dashboard or share with stakeholders.

## Data profiling

Before running an analysis, you can generate a profile of your data to understand what you are working with:

- Per-column statistics (averages, ranges, distributions)
- Missing data analysis
- Outlier detection
- Cardinality and uniqueness
- Overall data quality score

This helps you identify data quality issues before they affect your analysis results. See [Data Quality](/data/data-quality) for the full quality toolset.
